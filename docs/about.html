How the rankings work

Overview

These rankings are computed using a system called TGRS (Tennis Graph Ranking Score). It combines several independent signals — skill rating, strength of schedule, win graph reachability, and quality of wins — into a single number that attempts to reflect how strong a player or doubles pair truly is, even when they haven't played each other directly. The practical goal of TGRS is to estimate the seed a player is likely to receive at the MHSAA state tournament — it rewards not just skill but the breadth of competition and chain of wins that selectors consider when seeding.

Rankings are computed separately for Singles and Doubles, and within each MHSAA division (1–4). Flights 1 through 4 are all included.

Step 1 — TrueSkill Rating

Every player starts with a default skill estimate (μ = 25, σ = 8.33). After each match, both the winner's and loser's ratings are updated using the TrueSkill algorithm (Herbrich et al., 2007), a Bayesian skill rating system originally developed at Microsoft Research for Xbox Live matchmaking.

TrueSkill models each player's skill as a Gaussian distribution: μ (mu) is the estimated skill mean, and σ (sigma) is the uncertainty. A win against a strong opponent moves your μ up more than a win against a weak opponent. Over time, σ shrinks as more matches are played and the system becomes more confident in your rating.

Two TrueSkill models are computed for every player:

  • Global TrueSkill — trained on all matches in the same gender+category pool, ignoring division and flight. This gives a broad skill estimate that can compare players across divisions.
  • Local TrueSkill — trained only on matches within the same gender+category+division+flight bucket. This captures performance within the specific competitive context.

The conservative rating used for ranking is μ − 3σ, which represents a lower-bound confidence estimate. Players with few matches will have high σ and therefore a lower conservative rating, which reflects genuine uncertainty rather than poor performance. If you want a pure estimate of a player's skill independent of scheduling and graph structure, sorting by ts_rating (the conservative TrueSkill rating, μ − 3σ) is the most direct signal — it ignores who you've beaten and how connected you are, and simply asks: how good does this player appear to be based on match outcomes alone?

Step 2 — Win Graph & Reachability

Every match result is stored as a directed edge in a win graph: an arrow from the winner to the loser. When two players have played multiple times, only the most recent result is kept (so a rematch win updates the direction of the edge).

Reachability measures how many other players you can reach by following win-edges through the graph. If you beat player A, and player A beat player B, you can "reach" both A and B. A high reachability score means you are connected to a large portion of the competitive field through a chain of wins — even if you haven't played all of them directly.

  • Global reachability — counted across the entire gender+category pool, so cross-division wins count.
  • Local reachability — counted only within the same division+flight bucket.

Reachability is the single largest component of TGRS because it directly rewards beating players who themselves have strong records, and it is robust to scheduling differences between schools.

Step 3 — Strength of Schedule (SOS)

SOS is the average conservative TrueSkill rating of all opponents a player has faced. A player who consistently faces strong competition will have a higher SOS even if their win-loss record is modest.

Two versions are computed:

  • Global SOS — opponent ratings drawn from the global TrueSkill pool. Rewards playing outside your division.
  • Local SOS — opponent ratings drawn from the local bucket model. Reflects the quality of competition within your specific flight.

Step 4 — Quality Wins

Quality wins looks at the top 3 opponents you have beaten (by global TrueSkill μ) and takes their average. This rewards players who have at least a few wins against genuinely strong competition, rather than just accumulating wins against weak opponents.

Only direct wins count here — the global win graph is used so cross-division and cross-flight wins are included.

The TGRS Formula

All components are combined into a single weighted sum:

TGRS = w_global_mu × global_ts_mu + w_global_sos × global_sos + w_global_reach × global_reachability + w_quality_wins × quality_wins + w_local_sos × local_sos + w_local_mu × local_ts_mu + w_local_reach × local_reachability

The approximate weights for each component are:

ComponentWeightWhat it measures
global_ts_mu ~0.40 Your skill mean from the full gender+category TrueSkill model — the broadest skill signal
global_sos ~0.36 Average strength of all opponents you've faced, rated by the global model
global_reachability ~0.88 How many players you can reach through chains of wins across the whole pool — the largest single component
quality_wins ~0.41 Average global μ of your top 3 beaten opponents
local_sos ~0.40 Average strength of opponents within your specific division+flight bucket
local_ts_mu ~0.06 Your skill mean from the local bucket TrueSkill model
local_reachability ~0.50 How many players you can reach through wins within your own division+flight
Note: weights are tuned and may be adjusted over time as the system is refined. The values shown are approximate — the exact numbers are stored in graph_engine.py.

TGRS Scaled

The raw TGRS score is not easy to interpret on its own. To make it more readable, a TGRS Scaled score is also computed within each division+flight bucket:

  1. Find the 30th-ranked player's TGRS in the bucket and floor it to the nearest whole number.
  2. Subtract that value from every player's TGRS. Anyone ranked below 30th is clipped to 0.
  3. Scale the results so the top player in the bucket scores exactly 100.

This means a TGRS Scaled of 100 is the best player in the bucket, a score around 50 is roughly mid-tier among the top 30, and a score of 0 means the player ranked outside the top 30 by TGRS.

Team Rankings

Team scores are computed by awarding points to each school based on where their best-ranked player (or pair) finishes in each of the eight lineup slots: Singles flights 1, 2, 3, 4 and Doubles flights 1, 2, 3, 4. Only one entry per school per slot is counted — the highest-ranked player or pair from that school.

The point values by finishing position are:

FinishPoints
1st12.5
2nd10.0
3rd – 4th7.50
5th – 8th5.00
9th – 16th2.50
17th – 32nd1.00
33rd+0

Points are summed across all eight slots (Singles flights 1–4 and Doubles flights 1–4) for a maximum possible score of 100 (12.5 × 8). A school with no ranked player in a slot scores 0 for that slot. The structure mirrors a bracket seeding system — finishing first is worth significantly more than finishing second, and the gaps widen toward the top to reflect the difficulty of holding a top seed.

Note: team rankings are experimental. Because individual rankings require a minimum number of matches, some schools may be missing players in certain slots even if they competed in that flight.

Data & Limitations

Match data is crawled automatically from TennisReporting.com, which is the platform used by most Michigan high school tennis programs to report results. Rankings are updated daily at 4am EDT.

Known limitations:

  • A minimum number of matches is required to appear in rankings, so players early in the season or with few recorded matches may be absent.
  • Division assignments come from school metadata on TennisReporting. Some schools have unusual or missing division labels that get normalized to Division 4/Other. You can report corrections by opening an issue on the GitHub repository.
  • Doubles pairs are treated as a single entity. If the same two players appear in different pair combinations, each combination is ranked separately.
  • Tournament bracket matches and regular season matches are both included. Some matches may be double-counted if reported in both contexts — deduplication is applied where possible.
  • Rankings reflect results within a single season only. Each season starts from scratch with no carry-over.
